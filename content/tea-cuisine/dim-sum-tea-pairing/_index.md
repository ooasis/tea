# Dim Sum & Tea: The Perfect Pairing Guide

There's a reason dim sum is synonymous with tea. The Chinese phrase **yum cha** (飲茶) literally means "drink tea" — the tea isn't an afterthought, it's the whole point. The dim sum is what you eat *while* drinking tea.

But not all teas go with all dim sum. A delicate har gow needs something different than a hearty siu mai or a greasy spring roll. Here's how to pair like a pro.

## The Golden Rules of Dim Sum Tea Pairing

### 1. **Match intensity**
Light, delicate dumplings want light, fragrant teas. Rich, fatty dishes want teas that cut through grease.

### 2. **Consider the cooking method**
Steamed dim sum pairs best with subtle teas. Fried items need more assertive brews. Braised dishes can handle darker, earthier teas.

### 3. **Let the tea cleanse, not compete**
The tea's job is to refresh your palate between bites. It should complement, not overpower.

### 4. **Temperature matters**
Dim sum is served hot — room temperature or slightly warm tea works better than scalding tea, which can overwhelm delicate flavors.

## The Pairings

### 1. Siu Mai (燒賣) + Shou Mei White Tea 🥟

**Why it works:** Siu mai is rich — pork, shrimp, and mushrooms wrapped in a thin wonton skin. It's savory and slightly fatty. **Shou Mei** (寿眉, "Longevity Eyebrow") is an aged white tea with honeyed, slightly woody notes that cut through the richness without fighting it. Its mild astringency cleanses the palate between bites.

**Brewing tip:** Use 85°C water, steep 3 minutes. Let it cool slightly before sipping.

**Alternative:** A light oolong like Tieguanyin also works beautifully.

---

### 2. Har Gow (蝦餃) + Jasmine Green Tea 🦐

**Why it works:** Har gow are the aristocrats of the dim sum cart — translucent wrappers, plump shrimp, clean flavors. Nothing should interfere. **Jasmine green tea** is floral and delicate, matching the shrimp's sweetness without overwhelming it. The jasmine fragrance complements rather than masks.

**Brewing tip:** 80°C water, steep 2-3 minutes. Don't oversteep or it gets bitter.

**Alternative:** A premium Dragon Well (Longjing) green tea.

---

### 3. Char Siu Bao (叉燒包) + Oolong Tea 🐷

**Why it works:** Sweet BBQ pork in a fluffy steamed bun — char siu bao is a flavor bomb of sweet, savory, and umami. **Oolong tea** has the body to stand up to it. A medium-roasted oolong (like Dong Ding or Wuyi Rock Tea) brings a toasty, slightly fruity character that mirrors the caramelized pork.

**Brewing tip:** 90°C water, steep 1-2 minutes. Multiple infusions work great here.

**Alternative:** A rich black tea like Keemun.

---

### 4. Spring Rolls (春卷) + Pu-erh Tea 🥟

**Why it works:** Fried foods need a tea that cuts through oil. **Pu-erh** (ripe/aged) is earthy, smooth, and almost medicinal in its ability to reset your palate. It strips grease and prepares you for the next bite. This is the classic Cantonese dim sum pairing for a reason.

**Brewing tip:** Boiling water (100°C), quick steep — 20-30 seconds. Rinse the leaves first.

**Alternative:** Wuyi Rock Tea (Da Hong Pao) for a roasted, mineral kick.

---

### 5. Chicken Feet (鳳爪) + Lapsang Souchong 🐔

**Why it works:** Chicken feet are intensely flavored — fermented black bean sauce, garlic, chili, and that unique sticky, gelatinous texture. You need a tea with personality. **Lapsang Souchong** (正山小种) is a smoked black tea with a bold, campfire character that stands up to the intensity without backing down.

**Brewing tip:** 95°C water, steep 2 minutes. The smoke should be present but not overwhelming.

**Alternative:** A bold Yunnan black tea (Dian Hong).

---

### 6. Shrimp Rice Rolls (蝦腸粉) + Silver Needle White Tea 🥢

**Why it works:** Silky rice noodle rolls with shrimp are all about texture — soft, slippery, delicate. **Silver Needle** (白毫银针) is the lightest, most elegant white tea, with hay-like sweetness and virtually no astringency. It glides alongside the rice rolls like they were made for each other.

**Brewing tip:** 75-80°C water, steep 4-5 minutes. Be patient — this tea unfolds slowly.

**Alternative:** A very light green tea.

---

### 7. Turnip Cake (蘿蔔糕) + Tieguanyin 🥕

**Why it works:** Fried turnip cake is crispy on the outside, soft on the inside, with bits of Chinese sausage and dried shrimp. **Tieguanyin** (Iron Goddess of Mercy) is a lightly oxidized oolong with floral, creamy notes and a lingering sweetness that complements the savory, pan-fried cake beautifully.

**Brewing tip:** 90°C water, steep 45 seconds. Tieguanyin can be re-steeped 5-6 times.

---

### 8. Sticky Rice in Lotus Leaf (糯米雞) + Shui Xian Oolong 🍚

**Why it works:** Lotus leaf-wrapped sticky rice is a meal in itself — chicken, sausage, mushroom, egg, all steaming together in an aromatic leaf. **Shui Xian** (Narcissus) oolong is a rich, dark oolong with a mineral, slightly honeyed character that echoes the earthy, fragrant notes of the lotus leaf.

**Brewing tip:** 95°C water, steep 10 seconds first infusion, then gradually increase.

## One More Tip: The No-Brew Option

If you're at a busy dim sum house and don't want to fuss with gongfu brewing, just order **Jasmine tea**. It's the universal dim sum tea — floral enough for delicate dumplings, light enough for fried items, and forgiving enough to sit in a pot for 20 minutes without turning bitter.

It's the default for a reason.
