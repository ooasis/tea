**Oolong** is the “artisan’s category”: tea that is **partially oxidized** (often anywhere from ~20% to ~80%) and shaped by repeated, deliberate handling. The hallmark is aroma complexity—orchid and lilac in light styles, fruit and spice in medium styles, and deep roast and mineral in darker styles.

### The core craft

- **Wither** to soften the leaf
- **Bruise/shake** to trigger edge oxidation and aroma development
- **Rest** (multiple cycles) to steer oxidation
- **Fixation** to stop oxidation
- **Rolling/shaping**
- **Roast** (optional but common; from light to heavy charcoal)

### Two “worlds” of oolong you’ll encounter

- **Anxi (Fujian)**: fragrance-forward styles like Tieguanyin
- **Wuyi / Yancha (Fujian)**: “rock rhyme” (*yán yùn*)—mineral depth and roast
- **Phoenix (Guangdong)**: single-bush aromatics (*dancong*)
- **Taiwan**: high-mountain florals and creamy texture

### Famous varieties & origins

- **Tieguanyin (Iron Goddess of Mercy)** — Anxi, Fujian
- **Da Hong Pao (Big Red Robe)** — Wuyi Mountains, Fujian
- **Rougui (Cinnamon Oolong)** — Wuyi Mountains, Fujian
- **Shuixian (Narcissus Oolong)** — Wuyi Mountains, Fujian
- **Phoenix Dancong** — Phoenix Mountains, Guangdong
- **Alishan High Mountain Oolong** — Alishan, Taiwan
- **Dong Ding Oolong** — Dong Ding Mountain, Taiwan
- **Oriental Beauty (Bai Hao Oolong)** — Hsinchu & Miaoli, Taiwan
